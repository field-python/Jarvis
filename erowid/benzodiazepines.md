# Benzodiazepines
tags: depressant, GABA, benzo, anxiety, sedative, withdrawal
category: Depressants

## Quick Facts
| Field | Info |
|-------|------|
| Also known as | Benzos, bars, xans, Xanax, Valium, Klonopin, blues |
| Drug class | GABA-A positive allosteric modulator |
| Mechanism | Enhances GABA (inhibitory neurotransmitter) activity |
| Key drugs | Diazepam (Valium), alprazolam (Xanax), clonazepam (Klonopin), lorazepam (Ativan), temazepam, triazolam |
| Legal status (US) | Schedule IV (pharmaceutical); street benzos and designer benzos may be Schedule I |

## Overview
Benzodiazepines were developed in the 1950s (first: chlordiazepoxide/Librium 1955, diazepam/Valium 1963). They replaced barbiturates as the primary anxiolytic and sedative class because they have a much higher therapeutic index (harder to accidentally overdose on alone).

Widely prescribed for anxiety, panic disorder, insomnia, alcohol withdrawal, seizures, and muscle relaxation. Among the most prescribed drugs in the US.

Designer benzodiazepines (etizolam, flualprazolam, clonazolam, bromazolam, etc.) are sold on the grey market — many are Schedule I or unscheduled and may be more potent or longer-acting than pharmaceutical benzos. Often found in pressed pills sold as Xanax.

## Effects
**Therapeutic/desired:** Anxiolysis (anxiety reduction), sedation, muscle relaxation, anticonvulsant effect, euphoria (mild, more pronounced with fast-acting types), amnesia (lorazepam, triazolam)
**High doses:** Slurred speech, ataxia (loss of coordination), profound sedation, blackout/anterograde amnesia (can't form new memories while intoxicated)
**Respiratory:** Benzodiazepines alone rarely cause fatal respiratory depression (unlike opioids or barbiturates). The lethal risk is almost exclusively from combinations with other CNS depressants.

## Drug Combinations
| Combination | Safety | Notes |
|-------------|--------|-------|
| Alcohol | Dangerous | Both potentiate GABA — combined CNS/respiratory depression. Many benzo deaths involve alcohol. |
| Opioids | Dangerous | Major overdose risk. FDA black box warning. Extremely common fatal combination. |
| Barbiturates | Dangerous | Profound CNS depression |
| GHB | Dangerous | Synergistic CNS depression |
| Ketamine | Caution | CNS depressant synergy |
| Antidepressants | Caution | Complex interactions |
| Stimulants | Reduces | Stimulants mask benzo sedation — may lead to excessive benzodiazepine use |
| Psychedelics | Useful (rescue) | Benzos reduce or abort psychedelic experiences — use as rescue medication |

## Harm Reduction
**The most dangerous thing about benzos is the combination with other CNS depressants** (alcohol, opioids, GHB, barbiturates). Benzos alone, even at high doses, rarely cause death in healthy people. With alcohol or opioids, the risk is significant.

**Fake Xanax:** Most street Xanax is pressed pills containing designer benzos (flualprazolam, etizolam, clonazolam, bromazolam). These may be more potent, have different durations, and unknown risk profiles. Fentanyl has been found in pressed benzo pills. Test everything.

**Memory blackouts:** Alprazolam, triazolam, and many RC benzos cause anterograde amnesia at moderate doses. Users may function apparently normally during a blackout with no memory of events. Obvious dangers: accidents, unwanted sexual encounters, saying/doing things you won't remember.

**Dosing:** Space out doses. Know your source. Don't combine.

## Withdrawal — LIFE-THREATENING
**Benzodiazepine withdrawal is one of the few drug withdrawal syndromes that can be fatal.**

Unlike opioid withdrawal (uncomfortable but not dangerous), benzo withdrawal can cause grand mal seizures and death.

**Risk factors for severe withdrawal:** Daily use for more than 2–4 weeks, high doses, short-acting benzos (alprazolam), abrupt cessation.

**Do NOT stop cold turkey after regular use.** Taper must be slow.

**Withdrawal timeline (short-acting, e.g. alprazolam):**
- **6–24hr:** Anxiety, tremors, sweating, insomnia
- **1–3 days:** Peak — anxiety, nausea, vomiting, possible seizures
- **1–2 weeks:** Acute phase resolves (for short-acting)

**For long-acting (diazepam):**
- **1–7 days:** Onset of symptoms
- **2–4 weeks:** Peak symptoms
- **Months:** PAWS — anxiety, insomnia, cognitive issues

**Medical detox protocol:**
1. Substitute with long-acting benzo (diazepam preferred)
2. Slow taper: no more than 5–10% dose reduction every 1–2 weeks
3. Ashton Manual is the gold standard protocol for benzo tapering — free online
4. Inpatient setting for heavy long-term use

**Symptoms requiring immediate medical attention:** Seizures, hallucinations, extreme confusion, extreme agitation.

## Dependence vs Addiction
Physical dependence (your body adapts and needs the drug to function normally) can develop in just 2–4 weeks of daily use — even at therapeutic doses as prescribed. This is different from addiction. Many people become physically dependent after legitimate medical use.

Addiction (compulsive use despite negative consequences) is an additional but separate issue.
