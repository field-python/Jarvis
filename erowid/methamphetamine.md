## Overview
Methamphetamine was first synthesized in Japan in 1893 and used extensively in WWII by Allied and Axis forces to combat fatigue. Pervitin (meth) was issued to German soldiers as a standard part of their gear. Used medically post-war for obesity and ADHD.

The illicit market was initially biker gang-dominated (1970s–90s), then shifted to Mexican cartel production of extremely pure crystal meth. Current street purity is often 95%+.

Duration (8–16hr) and dopamine-flooding mechanism make it one of the most addictive substances known.

## Effects
**Stimulant effects:** Intense euphoria (especially smoked/IV), energy, hyperfocus, decreased appetite, decreased need for sleep, confidence, hypersexuality
**Physical:** Hyperthermia, tachycardia, hypertension, pupil dilation, dry mouth, jaw clenching, hyperactivity
**Negative acute:** Anxiety, paranoia (especially at high doses), rapid speech, repetitive behaviors
**After-effects/comedown:** Extreme fatigue, depression, hunger, sleeping for 12–24hr ("meth crash")
**Chronic:** Psychosis, paranoia, hallucinations (visual and auditory), extreme weight loss, "meth mouth" (severe dental decay), skin picking (from tactile hallucinations)

## Routes of Administration
**Smoking:** Fastest to lung = seconds to effect. Intense rush. Most common current route. Glass pipe heated from below until crystal vaporizes.

**Insufflation:** 3–5 min onset. Less intense but more sustained.

**IV:** Fastest onset, most intense rush. Highest addiction/overdose risk. Greatest bloodborne disease risk.

**Oral:** 30–60 min onset. Least intense. Tablet form (Yaba in Southeast Asia, Desoxyn medically).

## Duration Breakdown
Duration makes meth uniquely taxing: 8–16 hours of stimulation before crash.
- **Onset:** Seconds (smoked) to 30 min (oral)
- **Peak:** 2–4 hr
- **Plateau:** 4–10 hr
- **Crash:** Begins as drug clears; can last 24–48hr

## Pharmacology
Meth causes massive release of dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin. More potent than amphetamine at dopamine release. Also inhibits MAO (monoamine oxidase), preventing breakdown of released neurotransmitters. Crosses blood-brain barrier rapidly.

**Neurotoxicity:** Well-established in humans and animals. Destroys dopaminergic axon terminals in striatum and serotonergic terminals in cortex. Effects are dose- and frequency-dependent. Some recovery occurs with prolonged abstinence but not complete. Long-term users show reduced dopamine transporter availability on imaging scans — correlated with cognitive impairment, depression, anhedonia.

**Addiction:** Among the highest addiction potential of any drug. Dopamine flooding creates powerful conditioned cravings. Withdrawal produces profound dysphoria, anhedonia, fatigue — the brain's dopamine system is depleted.

**Psychosis:** Can occur even on first use in predisposed individuals. In heavy users, meth psychosis (paranoia, hallucinations, delusions) is common and may persist for weeks after stopping. Clinically indistinguishable from schizophrenia during the episode.

## Harm Reduction
**Sleep:** The single most important variable. Sleep deprivation amplifies psychosis, impairs decision-making, and worsens the subsequent crash. Force sleep after 24hr maximum. Sleep deprivation is a more serious health risk than the meth itself in acute use.

**Eat:** Methamphetamine suppresses appetite severely. Force food intake. Weight loss and malnutrition are serious long-term risks.

**Hydration:** Drink water regularly. Hyperthermia and dry mouth are significant.

**Dental care:** Meth mouth = combination of dry mouth (reduced enamel protection), teeth grinding, sugar cravings, and neglect. Brush teeth and stay hydrated. Sugar-free gum for dry mouth.

**Testing:** Reagent testing with Marquis (orange-brown = meth). Fentanyl strips.

**Skin:** Avoid picking at skin during paranoid episodes. Many users develop open sores from this.

**HIV/STI risk:** Meth dramatically increases sexual risk-taking. High rates of HIV, Hepatitis C, and other STIs in meth-using communities — especially relevant for chemsex (Tina) use.

**Psychosis protocol:** If psychosis begins — stop using, sleep, safe environment. Antipsychotics (haloperidol, quetiapine) effective for acute meth psychosis. Seek medical help.

## Addiction and Recovery
Methamphetamine use disorder has limited pharmaceutical treatment options (unlike opioids). What has evidence:
- **Contingency management:** Behavioral therapy with rewards for negative drug tests — strong evidence
- **CBT:** Cognitive behavioral therapy for cravings and triggers
- **Mirtazapine:** Some evidence for reducing meth use
- **Bupropion:** Modest evidence for reducing use
- **Naltrexone:** Being studied
- Crystal Meth Anonymous (CMA): 12-step program specific to meth
