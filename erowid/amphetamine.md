# Amphetamine (Speed / Adderall / Dexedrine)

tags: stimulant, amphetamine, adderall, dexedrine, speed, adhd, prescription
category: stimulant

## Quick Facts
- **Type:** CNS Stimulant / Empathogen (at higher doses)
- **Legal status:** Schedule II (USA); prescription required. Illegal without Rx.
- **Duration:** 4-8 hours (IR); 10-12 hours (XR)
- **Onset:** 20-60 minutes oral; 5-15 minutes insufflated
- **Forms:** Amphetamine sulfate (speed), dextroamphetamine (Dexedrine),
  mixed amphetamine salts (Adderall), lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse)

## Overview

Amphetamine is a phenethylamine class stimulant. It is distinct from
methamphetamine — while structurally similar, amphetamine does not cross the
blood-brain barrier as readily and has a somewhat different effect profile
and lower neurotoxicity profile at equivalent doses.

Prescribed as Adderall and Dexedrine for ADHD and narcolepsy. Recreationally
used for focus, energy, euphoria, and weight loss. Widely misused by students.

## Effects

### Therapeutic / Low Doses
- Improved focus and concentration
- Reduced fatigue; increased wakefulness
- Mild mood elevation
- Decreased appetite
- Slightly elevated heart rate and blood pressure

### Recreational Doses
- Strong euphoria and energy
- Increased sociability and talkativeness
- Reduced need for sleep
- Heightened confidence and sense of capability
- Strong focus on tasks (even mundane ones become engaging)
- At higher doses: empathogenic effects — increased warmth and connection
- Sexual effects: increased desire but impaired performance ("stimulant dick")

## Dosage (Oral, Immediate Release)

- **Threshold:** 5-10mg
- **Light:** 10-20mg
- **Common (therapeutic):** 20-40mg
- **Common (recreational):** 20-60mg
- **Strong:** 60-100mg
- **Heavy/risky:** 100mg+

*Insufflation lowers effective dose by ~50% but increases cardiovascular
strain and addiction potential.*

## Duration
- **IR (Adderall, generic):** 4-6 hours
- **XR (Adderall XR, Vyvanse):** 10-12 hours
- **Dexedrine spansule:** 8-10 hours
- **Comedown:** 4-8 hours of fatigue after peak

## Pharmacology

- **Reverses VMAT2** — forces dopamine, norepinephrine, and serotonin out of
  vesicles into the synapse, flooding it
- **Blocks reuptake** of dopamine and norepinephrine
- **Net effect:** massive increase in dopamine and norepinephrine in reward
  and arousal circuits; significant serotonin release at higher doses
- Dextroamphetamine (d-amp) is the more potent enantiomer; Adderall is 75%
  d-amp, 25% l-amp

## Drug Combinations

| Combination | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| MAOIs | **LETHAL** | Hypertensive crisis; death |
| Cocaine | HIGH | Cardiovascular overload; heart attack risk |
| MDMA | HIGH | Hyperthermia; cardiac strain; neurotoxicity |
| Alcohol | Moderate-high | Masks intoxication; dehydration; cardiotoxic |
| Cannabis | Low-moderate | Can cause anxiety and paranoia |
| Caffeine | Low-moderate | Additional cardiovascular strain |
| Psychedelics | Low | Can intensify and cause anxiety/paranoia |
| Antidepressants (SSRIs) | Moderate | Reduced effects; some interaction risks |

## Harm Reduction

- Stay hydrated but don't overdrink (amphetamine reduces thirst sensation)
- Eat — amphetamine suppresses appetite but your body still needs fuel
- Do not dose after early afternoon if you need sleep that night
- Vitamin C and other acidic foods decrease absorption; take 1+ hour apart
- Sleep deprivation from use compounds health risks significantly
- Take breaks — tolerance builds quickly; scheduled "off" days prevent this
- Magnesium supplements may reduce jaw clenching (bruxism)
- Avoid redosing more than once per session

## Addiction and Dependence

Amphetamine has significant addiction potential, particularly with frequent
recreational use. Physical dependence develops with regular use.

**Withdrawal:** Fatigue (often severe), hypersomnia, depression, increased
appetite, cognitive fog. Typically resolves within 1-2 weeks but post-acute
withdrawal depression can persist longer.

**Not directly medically dangerous** but psychologically difficult. Stimulant
use disorder responds to cognitive behavioral therapy.

## Long-Term Risks

Regular heavy use:
- Cardiovascular strain (heart disease, hypertension)
- Neurotoxicity at very high doses (mainly dopaminergic nerve terminal damage)
- Sleep disorders
- Psychiatric effects: paranoia, anxiety, in extreme cases stimulant psychosis
- Weight loss and nutritional deficiency
- Cognitive effects with heavy chronic use

## Comparison to Methamphetamine

Amphetamine and methamphetamine are pharmacologically similar but:
- Meth crosses the blood-brain barrier more readily (more potent, faster onset)
- Meth has higher neurotoxicity at equivalent doses
- Adderall / pharmaceutical amphetamine is far purer than street meth
- The real-world harm differential is more about dosing and purity than pharmacology
