## Overview
Opioids are a class of drugs that act on opioid receptors throughout the brain and body, producing analgesia, euphoria, sedation, and respiratory depression. Derived from or related to the opium poppy.

**Natural opiates:** Morphine, codeine (directly from poppy)
**Semi-synthetic:** Heroin (diacetylmorphine), oxycodone, hydrocodone, hydromorphone, buprenorphine
**Synthetic:** Fentanyl, methadone, tramadol, meperidine

The opioid epidemic (1990s–present) has killed hundreds of thousands of Americans — initially driven by over-prescription of oxycodone (OxyContin), then shifted to heroin, then dominated by illicitly manufactured fentanyl (IMF) and fentanyl analogs.

## Effects
**Euphoria:** Intense warm rush (especially IV heroin/fentanyl), profound sense of well-being and contentment
**Analgesia:** Powerful pain relief
**Sedation:** Drowsiness, nodding
**Physical:** Nausea (especially initial use), constipation, dry mouth, itching (histamine release), miosis (pinpoint pupils), slowed breathing
**Respiratory depression:** The primary mechanism of overdose. At overdose doses, breathing slows to a stop.

## Individual Drugs

### Heroin (Diacetylmorphine)
Prodrug — converted to morphine in the brain. Crosses blood-brain barrier faster than morphine due to lipophilicity = more intense rush.
- **Routes:** IV (most intense, fastest), smoking, insufflation, rectal
- **Duration (IV):** 4–6 hr
- **Relative potency:** ~2x morphine

### Fentanyl
Synthetic opioid. 50–100x more potent than morphine. Originally for severe pain/anesthesia. Most street drug deaths now involve illicitly manufactured fentanyl (IMF).
- **Duration:** Shorter than heroin — 30–60 min (but respiratory depression outlasts euphoria)
- **Lethal dose margin:** Extremely narrow — 2mg can be lethal to a non-tolerant person
- **Carfentanil:** 100x more potent than fentanyl — used for large animals, has appeared in drug supply

### Oxycodone (OxyContin, Percocet)
Semi-synthetic. Oral tablets — often crushed and insufflated or dissolved and injected.
- **Duration:** 4–6 hr (IR), 8–12 hr (ER)
- **Common doses:** 5–10mg oral (opioid-naive), much higher for tolerant users

### Morphine
The prototypical opioid. Less lipophilic than heroin = slower onset, less intense rush.
- **Medical use:** IV/IM/oral for severe pain, end-of-life care
- **Duration:** 4–6 hr

### Codeine
Prodrug — converted to morphine by CYP2D6 enzyme. 10% of people (poor metabolizers) get almost no effect. Ultra-rapid metabolizers get dangerously high morphine levels.
- **Common dose:** 15–60mg
- **Often combined with:** Acetaminophen or ibuprofen (limits dosing due to acetaminophen toxicity)

### Methadone
Long-acting synthetic opioid. Used for OUD (opioid use disorder) treatment and chronic pain. Extremely long half-life (24–36hr) with large individual variation. Accumulates — risk of overdose during induction if dose escalated too fast.

### Buprenorphine (Suboxone, Subutex)
Partial agonist — ceiling effect on respiratory depression makes it far safer than full agonists. Used for OUD treatment. Contains naloxone in Suboxone to deter injection.

## Overdose
**Signs:**
- Unresponsive, cannot be awakened
- Slow, shallow, or absent breathing
- Gurgling/snoring sound (death rattle)
- Blue lips, fingertips (cyanosis)
- Pinpoint pupils

**What to do:**
1. Call 911 immediately
2. Administer naloxone (Narcan):
   - Nasal: Insert in one nostril, press plunger. Repeat other nostril if needed.
   - IM: 0.4mg into muscle (thigh/upper arm)
3. Rescue breathing if person not breathing
4. Recovery position if breathing returns
5. Naloxone wears off in 30–90 min — shorter than most opioids. Monitor and redose if needed.
6. Do NOT leave the person alone

**Good Samaritan laws:** Most states have laws protecting people who call 911 for an overdose from prosecution. Know your state's law.

## Harm Reduction

**Never use alone.** Most opioid deaths are solo. Never Use Alone hotline: 1-800-484-3731 — a live person stays on the phone and calls 911 if you stop responding.

**Naloxone:** Have it. Always. Free at most pharmacies without prescription in most states. Keep in reach, make sure people around you know where it is and how to use it.

**Fentanyl test strips:** Use them on EVERY batch. Fentanyl is in heroin, counterfeit pills, cocaine, MDMA — everywhere in the current supply. Dissolve a small amount in water and test. One line = fentanyl present.

**Tolerance resets rapidly.** After any period of abstinence (even days), your tolerance drops dramatically. Prior dose = potential fatal dose. Always start with a test dose after a break.

**Don't mix.** Opioids + benzodiazepines, alcohol, ketamine, GHB — all potentiate respiratory depression. This combination is responsible for a large majority of opioid deaths.

**Rotate injection sites.** If injecting: use clean needles every time (free at harm reduction programs/syringe service programs), avoid cotton fever (use sterile water), avoid hitting arteries.

**Test dose technique:** Use a small test amount from a new batch to assess potency before using your normal amount.

## Withdrawal
Opioid withdrawal is extremely uncomfortable but generally NOT life-threatening (unlike alcohol or benzo withdrawal). Timeline (short-acting opioids):
- **6–24hr:** Anxiety, yawning, sweating, runny nose, muscle aches
- **24–72hr:** Peak — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, severe muscle cramps, insomnia, agitation, goosebumps ("cold turkey" = goosebumps)
- **7–10 days:** Acute symptoms resolve
- **Weeks–months:** PAWS (post-acute withdrawal syndrome) — low energy, anhedonia, cravings, sleep disturbance

**Medical treatment:** Buprenorphine significantly reduces withdrawal. Methadone for severe dependence. Clonidine (non-opioid) for autonomic symptoms. Loperamide for GI symptoms. Comfort medications (NSAIDs, antiemetics).

**Medications for OUD (MOUD):**
- **Buprenorphine/naloxone (Suboxone):** Highly effective, can be prescribed by any certified physician
- **Methadone:** Daily clinic dosing, federally regulated
- **Naltrexone (Vivitrol):** Opioid blocker, monthly injection, requires being opioid-free first
