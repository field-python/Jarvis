## Overview
Ketamine was developed in 1962 by Parke-Davis as an anesthetic. It is widely used in veterinary medicine, battlefield surgery, and pediatric anesthesia. It became popular recreationally in the 1970s and 1980s.

Unlike most psychedelics, ketamine has a legitimate pharmaceutical form (Ketalar) and is on the WHO's list of essential medicines. Esketamine (Spravato) is an FDA-approved nasal spray for treatment-resistant depression.

At sub-anesthetic doses, it produces dissociation, analgesia, and psychedelic-like effects. At high doses, users experience the "K-hole" — a state of profound dissociation from physical reality, similar in some ways to a DMT breakthrough.

## Effects
**Low doses:** Mild dissociation, mood lift, slight visual distortion, analgesia, increased sociability
**Moderate doses:** Strong dissociation, feeling of separation from body, perceptual distortions, confusion
**K-hole (high dose):** Complete dissociation from physical reality — near-death experiences, interdimensional experiences, inability to communicate or move; similar reports to DMT breakthrough but more inward and less visually complex
**Physical:** Analgesia (pain relief), increased heart rate, nausea (especially with oral), confusion, discoordination

IM peaks in 5–10 min. Shorter overall but intense. IV is fastest and most precise — medical setting.

## Pharmacology
Ketamine is a non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonist. It blocks glutamate (the brain's main excitatory neurotransmitter) from binding to NMDA receptors, causing dissociation. It also has opioid receptor activity, sigma receptor agonism, and monoamine reuptake inhibition — a complex pharmacological profile.

**Antidepressant mechanism:** Rapid glutamatergic antidepressant effects (within hours) via AMPA receptor potentiation and BDNF release — completely different from SSRIs. Hence the interest in treatment-resistant depression.

**Tolerance:** Develops rapidly with frequent use. Can become physically and psychologically habit-forming — unusual among psychedelics.

## Harm Reduction
**Bladder:** The most serious chronic risk. Ketamine cystitis — fibrosis and shrinkage of the bladder from frequent heavy use. Causes severe pain, frequent urination, blood in urine. May require bladder removal in extreme cases. **Reduce or stop use at first urinary symptoms.** Staying well-hydrated helps; more frequent lower doses rather than infrequent high doses is less damaging.

**Ketamine addiction:** Unlike classic psychedelics, ketamine has significant addiction potential. Daily use leads to psychological dependence and tolerance that requires increasing doses. Set clear limits.

**K-hole safety:** K-holes are not inherently dangerous physically if the person is in a safe horizontal position. However: do not use near water (drowning risk), do not stand near ledges, stairs, or traffic. Have a sitter.

**Do not mix with depressants.** Alcohol, opioids, benzodiazepines, GHB — all dangerous in combination.

**Cardiovascular:** Ketamine is a cardiac stimulant at sub-anesthetic doses. Caution with heart conditions.

## Medical Uses
- Battlefield/emergency anesthetic (widely used for this — does not suppress breathing at anesthetic doses, unlike other anesthetics)
- Pediatric anesthesia
- Chronic pain management
- Treatment-resistant depression (IV ketamine infusions, esketamine nasal spray)
- Rapidly reducing suicidal ideation (hours, vs weeks for SSRIs)
