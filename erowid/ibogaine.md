## Overview
Ibogaine is the principal psychoactive alkaloid from the iboga plant, used for centuries in the Bwiti religion of Gabon and Cameroon as a sacrament for initiation rites. The full initiation ceremony involves very high doses producing a 24-36hr visionary experience believed to allow the initiate to meet their ancestors and receive guidance.

In the West, ibogaine became notable in the 1960s when Howard Lotsof accidentally discovered its apparent ability to interrupt opioid addiction — he used it recreationally and woke up 30 hours later with dramatically reduced withdrawal and cravings. He spent decades advocating for research.

Ibogaine treatment for addiction (opioid use disorder, stimulant addiction, alcohol use disorder) is offered at clinics in Mexico, Portugal, South Africa, New Zealand, and other countries. Research is ongoing in the US despite Schedule I status.

**Cardiac risk** makes ibogaine the most medically significant psychedelic from a safety standpoint.

## Effects
Ibogaine produces an experience unlike any other psychedelic:

**Phase 1 — Acute (0–8hr):** Intense, panoramic visions. Often described as "watching a movie of your life" — life review, memory recall with unusual vividness. May be challenging and confrontational. Ataxia (inability to walk, remain stationary required).

**Phase 2 — Evaluative (8–20hr):** Dream-like state. Processing of visions. Introspective.

**Phase 3 — Residual stimulation (20–36hr):** Can't sleep, subtle stimulation, integration.

**Physical:** Severe nausea and vomiting common. Ataxia. Eye movements. Tachycardia. Cardiac arrhythmia risk (significant — see harm reduction).

**Anti-addiction mechanism:** Ibogaine interrupts opioid receptor pathways, reduces withdrawal symptoms dramatically, and resets tolerance. Duration of reduced cravings varies widely — some people report weeks to months; others relapse quickly. Works best with integration and aftercare support.

## Dosage
| Use | Dose (ibogaine HCl) | Notes |
|-----|---------------------|-------|
| Microdose | 1–5mg | Sub-perceptual, used for mood/micro-treatment |
| Flood dose | 10–15mg/kg | Standard addiction treatment dose |
| Ceremonial | 5–10mg/kg | Shorter visionary experience |

**Must be dosed by weight.** Individual variation is significant. Medical setting required for flood doses.

## Pharmacology
Complex. Ibogaine:
- NMDA receptor antagonist (similar to ketamine — dissociative component)
- Kappa-opioid agonist
- Sigma-2 receptor agonist
- Blocks serotonin, dopamine, and norepinephrine reuptake
- The active metabolite noribogaine has longer duration and is thought responsible for much of the anti-addictive effect

**Cardiac effect:** Ibogaine blocks hERG potassium channels in the heart — causes QT interval prolongation. This can trigger potentially fatal cardiac arrhythmias (torsades de pointes). **This is the primary danger.** Pre-existing cardiac conditions dramatically increase risk. EKG required before use.

## Harm Reduction — CARDIAC RISK IS SERIOUS
**Ibogaine has caused deaths — primarily from cardiac events.**

**Pre-treatment requirements:**
- EKG: QTc must be within normal range (<450ms). Any prolongation = contraindicated.
- Full cardiac workup for anyone with cardiac history
- No QT-prolonging drugs (many antidepressants, antipsychotics, antibiotics — see full list)
- Opioid-abstinent (partial — reduce to avoid precipitated withdrawal but not fully abstinent)
- Blood chemistry: liver function, electrolytes (low potassium/magnesium increases cardiac risk)
- No concurrent medications unless cleared by physician

**During treatment:**
- Cardiac monitoring (EKG) throughout is standard of care
- Crash cart and medical personnel on site
- IV fluids
- Lying down for majority of session (ataxia + vomiting risk if upright)

**Contraindications:**
- Heart disease, arrhythmias, family history of sudden cardiac death
- QT prolongation on baseline EKG
- Liver disease (ibogaine is metabolized by liver)
- Seizure history
- Active psychiatric disorder (schizophrenia, bipolar I)
- Most psychiatric medications

**Death risk:** Estimated at 1 in 300–400 treatment episodes at irresponsible clinics. At properly screened, medically monitored clinics: dramatically lower. All reported ibogaine deaths had at least one contraindication.

## Addiction Treatment Context
**Best evidence for:**
- Opioid use disorder (heroin, fentanyl, prescription opioids) — interrupts withdrawal, resets tolerance, reduces cravings
- Stimulant addiction (cocaine, methamphetamine)
- Alcohol use disorder

**Less evidence for:** Tobacco, cannabis

**One flood dose** can eliminate acute opioid withdrawal symptoms. Cravings reduction is variable. Integration and aftercare are essential — ibogaine by itself without behavioral follow-up has much higher relapse rates.

**Cost:** US-accessible clinics in Mexico run $5,000–$15,000 for a full treatment. Cost is a significant access barrier.

## Cultural Context
- Bwiti religion (Gabon, Cameroon, Equatorial Guinea) — iboga is central sacrament
- Initiation ceremony involves multi-day iboga use under guidance of ngangas (healers)
- Bwiti practitioners are recognized as the traditional keepers of iboga knowledge
- Iboga plant is listed on CITES Appendix II — international trade regulated (endangered)
- Ethical debate around sourcing — wild iboga is threatened; cultivated sources preferred
