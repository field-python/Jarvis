## Overview
Cocaine is an alkaloid extracted from the leaves of the coca plant, used by Andean peoples for thousands of years. The plant leaves are chewed or brewed as tea (coca tea) — a mild stimulant effect similar to caffeine, used for altitude sickness, energy, and hunger suppression.

Cocaine hydrochloride (powder) was first isolated in 1860. Freud wrote enthusiastically about its medicinal uses. It was an ingredient in early Coca-Cola (trace amounts). Widely used medically through the early 1900s. Scheduled as Schedule II in 1970 (still has legitimate medical use as a topical anesthetic in ENT surgery).

**Crack cocaine:** Cocaine HCl converted to freebase form by adding baking soda and water, cooking until solid. Smoked — intense, rapid onset, very short duration. More addictive due to speed of onset.

## Effects
- Intense euphoria, confidence, energy, talkativeness
- Appetite suppression
- Decreased need for sleep
- Increased heart rate and blood pressure
- Vasoconstriction (cold extremities, pallor)
- Local anesthetic effect (numbing)
- Pupil dilation
- **Comedown:** Crashing — fatigue, depression, irritability, cravings

## Duration by Route
| Route | Onset | Duration |
|-------|-------|----------|
| Insufflated | 3–5 min | 30–60 min |
| IV | Seconds | 15–30 min |
| Smoked (crack) | Seconds | 10–20 min |
| Oral (chewing leaves) | 30–60 min | 1–2 hr |

The shorter the duration, the higher the addiction potential. Crack's brief, intense high drives compulsive redosing.

## Pharmacology
Cocaine blocks the reuptake of dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine. Primarily known as a dopamine reuptake inhibitor — dopamine floods the nucleus accumbens (reward center), producing euphoria. The dopamine depletion after the high causes the crash and cravings.

Also a sodium channel blocker (local anesthetic mechanism) — vasoconstriction, numbness.

**Addiction:** Very high addiction liability. Rapid dopamine flooding creates strong conditioned associations. Withdrawal involves intense cravings, dysphoria, fatigue — no physical danger but psychologically difficult.

## Harm Reduction
**Cardiovascular:** Primary acute risk. Cocaine is a significant cardiac stressor. Vasoconstriction + tachycardia + hypertension. Risk of heart attack and stroke even in young healthy users, especially with pre-existing conditions. Do not use with heart conditions.

**Nasal damage:** Chronic insufflation causes mucosal damage, septal perforation. Rinse nostrils with saline after use. Use clean straws, never share (bloodborne disease risk). Allow nasal recovery time between uses.

**Cocaethylene:** Mixing with alcohol creates cocaethylene — extends the high but is more cardiotoxic than either substance alone. Common combination but significantly more dangerous than cocaine alone.

**Levamisole:** Most cocaine supply (70%+) is cut with levamisole, an antiparasitic drug. Long-term use causes agranulocytosis (failure of white blood cell production) — serious immune suppression. Regular CBC blood tests if using frequently.

**Fentanyl test strips:** Cocaine has been found to be contaminated with fentanyl. Test all batches.

**Set limits before you start.** Cocaine's compulsive redosing is a defining feature. Pre-decide how much you'll use and stop there.

## Physical Health Risks
- Cardiovascular: heart attack, stroke, aortic dissection (with heavy use)
- Nasal: mucosal atrophy, septum perforation, loss of smell
- Lung (smoked): crack lung, respiratory damage
- Weight loss, malnutrition with heavy use
- Dental damage (dry mouth, teeth grinding)
- Levamisole-related agranulocytosis (contamination)
- Infectious disease from IV use or sharing equipment
