## Overview
Mescaline is the primary psychoactive alkaloid in peyote (Lophophora williamsii), a small spineless cactus native to the Chihuahuan Desert of Mexico and Texas, and in various Trichocereus cacti (San Pedro, Peruvian torch) of South America. Indigenous ceremonial use of peyote dates back at least 5,700 years.

Mescaline was first isolated by German chemist Arthur Heffter in 1897 and first synthesized by Ernst Späth in 1919. It was the first psychedelic to be chemically characterized. Huxley's famous essay "The Doors of Perception" (1954) documents a mescaline experience.

Mescaline is unique among major psychedelics as a phenethylamine (same class as dopamine, amphetamine) rather than a tryptamine. Despite different chemistry, it produces strikingly similar experiences to psilocybin/LSD.

## Effects
**Visual:** Rich, colorful geometric patterns, especially with eyes closed. Vivid, saturated colors. The "mescaline aesthetic" is noted for its beautiful, jewel-like quality. Less reality-distorting than LSD; more dreamlike than psilocybin.
**Cognitive:** Profound empathy, connection to nature, introspective depth, philosophical thinking
**Emotional:** Warm, open-hearted; less likely to produce fear/anxiety than LSD at comparable intensity
**Physical:** Nausea (especially from raw cactus preparation), heightened sensory sensitivity, some cardiovascular stimulation
**Notable:** Often described as warmer and more "earthy" than LSD; deeper emotional access; described by many as a healing experience

## Dosage (Mescaline HCl or Sulfate)
| Level | Dose | Effects |
|-------|------|---------|
| Threshold | 100–150mg | Very subtle |
| Light | 150–200mg | Gentle visuals, mood lift |
| Common | 200–300mg | Full experience |
| Strong | 300–500mg | Intense, ego dissolution |
| Heavy | 500mg+ | Very intense, not recommended for beginners |

**Peyote buttons:** Approximately 1–1.5% mescaline content. A "common" dose = 15–20 dried peyote buttons (3–5g dried weight). Extremely bitter. Nausea is common.

**San Pedro cactus:** Approximately 0.1–2% mescaline (highly variable). Requires preparation (skinning, removing pulp, long simmering, reducing to a liquid or paste). 30cm of 10cm-diameter cactus is a rough starting point.

**Synthetic mescaline:** Most precise dosing. Clean experience without plant material nausea.

## Pharmacology
Mescaline is a 5-HT2A agonist (same mechanism as tryptamine psychedelics) despite being a phenethylamine. It also has weaker effects at 5-HT2C, 5-HT2B, and dopamine receptors. No physical toxicity at standard doses. LD50 in animals is very high relative to active dose.

Cross-tolerant with LSD and psilocybin (same 5-HT2A mechanism). Tolerance resets in 3–7 days.

## Preparation (San Pedro / Peruvian Torch)
1. Remove spines, then use a knife or peeler to remove the outer green skin + green layer below it (this is where mescaline concentrates — save everything)
2. Discard the white pulpy core (low alkaloid content)
3. Blend the green material into a slurry
4. Add water, simmer at low heat for 4–6 hours, stirring frequently
5. Strain and press out plant matter
6. Reduce the liquid until concentrated (dark green/brown thick liquid)
7. Drink the reduction — very bitter. Mix with juice to help.

**Citric acid method (precipitation):**
After reduction, add citric acid to create mescaline citrate salt — can dry into a powder for more precise dosing.

## Harm Reduction
- **Nausea management:** Nausea is common especially from raw plant material. Ginger tea, empty stomach (4-6hr fast), anti-nausea medication (not ones that block serotonin). The nausea passes within 1-2hr.
- **Duration:** 10-12hr is long — commit fully, clear your schedule
- **Set and setting:** Mescaline has a history of outdoor/nature use — many users find it pairs exceptionally well with natural settings
- **Integration:** Mescaline experiences are often reported as profoundly healing; integration journaling is valuable

## Cultural/Historical Context
- Peyote use in Native American ceremony dates back thousands of years
- Native American Church (NAC) members have legal protection for peyote sacramental use in the US
- Peyote cactus is endangered in the wild due to overharvesting and habitat loss — ethical considerations around wild peyote use
- San Pedro has been used in Andean healing traditions (curanderismo) for 3,000+ years
- Huxley's *The Doors of Perception* (1954) introduced mescaline to Western popular culture
