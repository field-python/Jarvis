## Overview
GHB (gamma-hydroxybutyrate) is an endogenous substance (occurs naturally in the brain as a neurotransmitter) and a CNS depressant. GBL (gamma-butyrolactone) is a prodrug that converts to GHB in the body almost immediately. GBL is more potent and faster-acting than GHB.

Used recreationally for euphoria, sociability, and aphrodisiac effects. Popular in chemsex contexts. Also used (and abused) in bodybuilding for GH release claims.

GHB was briefly FDA-approved as an anesthetic and is still used (as Xyrem/sodium oxybate) for narcolepsy/cataplexy.

The combination of narrow dosing window, variable concentration in solutions, and synergy with alcohol makes GHB one of the most acutely dangerous recreational drugs.

## Effects
**Low doses (0.5–1g):** Mild relaxation, sociability, mild euphoria, reduced inhibitions
**Common doses (1–2.5g):** Significant euphoria, sexual arousal, relaxation, slight sedation
**High doses (2.5–4g+):** Heavy sedation, nausea, confusion, unconsciousness
**Overdose:** Loss of consciousness, vomiting while unconscious (aspiration risk), respiratory depression

**The experience is highly dose-dependent.** The difference between a "nice dose" and "passed out and vomiting" is often less than 0.5–1g.

## Dosage
**CRITICAL: GHB solutions vary wildly in concentration.** Unknown street solutions cannot be safely dosed without testing.

| Level | GHB Dose | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Threshold | 0.5g | Very subtle |
| Light | 0.5–1.5g | Mild effects |
| Common | 1–2.5g | Full effects |
| Strong | 2.5–4g | Significant sedation risk |
| Overdose range | 4g+ | Unconsciousness for most non-tolerant users |

**GBL:** ~30–40% more potent than GHB by weight. 1g GBL ≈ 1.3–1.5g GHB effect.

**Redosing:** Many users redose too early (GHB's onset can be delayed 30+ min). Waiting for the first dose to kick in, then redosing — then both hit simultaneously — is a primary cause of accidental overdose.

## Duration Breakdown
- **Onset:** 15–30 min (can be up to 45 min with food)
- **Peak:** 30–60 min
- **Duration:** 1.5–3 hr
- **Short duration drives redosing** — and redosing drives most overdoses

## Drug Combinations
| Combination | Safety | Notes |
|-------------|--------|-------|
| Alcohol | Dangerous | Synergistic CNS depression. Even a single drink dramatically increases overdose risk. Most GHB deaths involve alcohol. |
| Benzodiazepines | Dangerous | CNS depressant synergy |
| Opioids | Dangerous | Respiratory depression |

| MDMA | Caution | Very popular "G + M" combination. Reduced overdose risk from MDMA's stimulant effect but still significant. |

## Harm Reduction
**Never combine with alcohol.** This single rule prevents most GHB deaths.

**Measure precisely.** Use a milligram-accurate scale or graduated syringe. Do not estimate.

**Know your solution's concentration.** Unknown street GHB cannot be safely used without concentration testing (which is difficult without lab equipment). Start with a tiny test amount and wait 45 minutes.

**Redosing rules:** Wait until AFTER peak effects before deciding to redose. Never redose sooner than 2 hours. Many people who overdose felt "nothing was happening" and redosed.

**Do not use alone.** With a sitter who knows what you've taken.

**Aspiration risk:** If unconscious, place in recovery position (on side) to prevent aspiration of vomit. Loss of consciousness + vomiting = choking risk.

**Tolerance and withdrawal:**
GHB/GBL withdrawal is DANGEROUS — similar to alcohol and benzo withdrawal, can cause seizures and death. Daily heavy users who stop abruptly can have life-threatening withdrawal within 12 hours. Medical supervision required for heavy daily users.

**Recovery position:** If someone is unresponsive from GHB — place them on their side, never on their back. Call 911. Do not leave them.

## Withdrawal (Heavy Daily Users)
- **Onset:** 4–8hr after last dose
- **Symptoms:** Anxiety, tremors, sweating, tachycardia, insomnia, confusion, hallucinations, seizures
- **Peak:** 24–72hr
- **Can be fatal without medical management**
- Treatment: GABA-ergic medications (benzodiazepines, barbiturates, baclofen); hospital setting
