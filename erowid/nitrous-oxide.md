# Nitrous Oxide (N2O)

tags: dissociative, inhalant, legal, whippits, laughing gas, nos, n2o
category: dissociative

## Quick Facts
- **Type:** Dissociative inhalant / anesthetic
- **Legal status:** Legal to purchase; varies by jurisdiction for recreational use
- **Duration:** 1-5 minutes (extremely short)
- **Onset:** Seconds
- **Route:** Inhalation only

## Overview

Nitrous oxide (N2O), also called laughing gas, whippits, or NOS, is a
dissociative gas used medically as an anesthetic and analgesic, and recreationally
for its brief euphoric, dissociative effects. It is one of the oldest known
recreational drugs, having been used at "laughing gas parties" in the early
1800s. It is commonly found in whipped cream chargers (8g cartridges) and
larger commercial tanks.

## Effects
- Intense euphoria (brief, often described as the best feeling imaginable)
- Dissociation and altered sense of reality
- Time dilation — seconds feel much longer
- Auditory and visual distortions; "wah wah" sound characteristic
- Laughter and giddiness
- Tingling sensations throughout body
- Temporary pain relief
- Brief loss of motor control
- After effects: mild headache, dizziness, potentially nausea

At very high doses: full dissociative "hole" comparable to ketamine

## Dosage

- **Threshold:** One 8g charger
- **Light:** 1-2 chargers
- **Common:** 2-4 chargers
- **Heavy:** 4+ chargers / balloons
- **Duration per hit:** 1-3 minutes
- **Medical (dental):** 30-70% concentration in oxygen

## Pharmacology

Nitrous oxide primarily works by:
- **NMDA glutamate receptor antagonism** — same mechanism as ketamine, DXM, PCP
- **Opioid receptor activation** — contributes to analgesia and euphoria
- **GABA-A receptor modulation** — mild enhancement
- **Dopamine release** — contributes to the euphoric rush

The extremely short duration is because N2O is eliminated through the lungs
within minutes of stopping inhalation.

## Drug Combinations

| Combination | Risk | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Cannabis | Low | Very common; intensifies dissociation and euphoria |
| Psychedelics (LSD/mushrooms) | Low-moderate | Can greatly intensify trip briefly |
| Ketamine | Moderate | Strong combined dissociation; use care |
| Alcohol | Moderate | Increased dizziness, nausea, falls risk |
| Opioids | Moderate | Respiratory depression possible |
| Other depressants | Moderate | Additive sedation |

## Harm Reduction

**Hypoxia is the primary acute risk.** N2O displaces oxygen.

- **Always breathe from a balloon**, never directly from a charger/tank — the
  pressure can rupture lungs and the cold gas can freeze the throat/lungs
- Never use a mask or bag that prevents you from pulling it away
- Always use sitting down or on the ground — people fall and injure themselves
- Allow fresh air breaths between hits — do not continuously rebreathe
- Never use alone (brief unconsciousness possible)
- Do not use while driving or operating anything
- Stay hydrated; avoid on an empty stomach to reduce nausea
- **Do not use more than once a week** to prevent B12 depletion

## Key Danger: Vitamin B12 Depletion

This is the most significant health risk from regular use. Nitrous oxide
irreversibly oxidizes (inactivates) vitamin B12. B12 is essential for myelin
sheath production — the protective coating of nerves.

**Heavy or frequent use can cause:**
- Subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord — permanent nerve damage
- Peripheral neuropathy — tingling, numbness, weakness in hands and feet
- Cognitive impairment
- Depression and psychiatric symptoms

**Risk factors that increase danger:**
- Pre-existing B12 deficiency (vegans/vegetarians at higher risk)
- Using folate supplements WITHOUT B12 (accelerates nerve damage)
- Heavy use (hundreds of cartridges at once / daily use)

**Prevention:** B12 supplementation (methylcobalamin form preferred).
Do not take folate supplements if using N2O regularly.

**This damage can be permanent.** Many people have developed lasting neurological
problems from extended heavy N2O use. The substance is far less benign than
its reputation suggests for frequent users.

## Long-Term Risks
- B12 deficiency / neuropathy (see above — the major risk)
- Tinnitus with heavy use
- Frostbite of lips/throat if using chargers without balloon
- Psychological habituation (not physically addictive but can be compulsive)

## Notes

N2O is found naturally in the atmosphere in trace amounts and is also a
greenhouse gas. Medical-grade N2O used in dentistry and surgery is delivered
at precise concentrations with supplemental oxygen — very different from
recreational use.
