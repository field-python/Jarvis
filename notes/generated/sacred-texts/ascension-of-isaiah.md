# Ascension of Isaiah

*Ethiopian Orthodox canon; considered pseudepigraphal by other traditions*
*Tags: ethiopian, deuterocanon, isaiah, vision, martyrdom, apocalyptic, ge'ez*

## Overview

The Ascension of Isaiah is a composite Jewish-Christian text preserved most
completely in the Ge'ez (Ethiopic) manuscripts of the Ethiopian Orthodox
tradition. It is one of the texts that the Ethiopian church holds as fully
canonical and which has no canonical status in any other surviving Christian
tradition.

The text is divided into two main parts: the Martyrdom of Isaiah and the
Vision (or Ascension) of Isaiah. Scholars believe these may originally have
been separate works later combined.

## Part One: The Martyrdom of Isaiah (Chapters 1–5)

### Setting

The narrative opens during the reign of King Hezekiah of Judah and continues
into the reign of his son Manasseh, who is depicted as deeply wicked and under
the influence of demonic forces, particularly a figure called Beliar (Belial),
the prince of evil.

### The Prophet in Danger

Isaiah is shown prophesying the coming of the Beloved (Christ) and the details
of his life, ministry, crucifixion, and resurrection with remarkable
specificity. These prophecies are said to enrage the false prophet Balkira
(sometimes called Belkira or Bechira), who accuses Isaiah before King Manasseh.

### Isaiah's Death

Isaiah is arrested and condemned. He is sawed in half with a wooden saw while
continuing to speak in the Holy Spirit, comforting those around him and
refusing to recant or beg for his life. His death is one of the most vivid
martyrdom accounts in all of ancient literature.

This tradition connects to the brief reference in Hebrews 11:37 — "they were
sawn asunder" — which many scholars believe refers to exactly this tradition
about Isaiah's death.

## Part Two: The Vision of Isaiah (Chapters 6–11)

### The Journey Through the Heavens

The second half is a remarkable apocalyptic vision. During the reign of
Hezekiah, Isaiah is caught up in a heavenly vision guided by an angel. He
travels through seven heavens, each higher and more glorious than the last.

**First through Fifth Heavens:** Isaiah encounters angelic beings, some
worshipping, some still bearing the marks of their earthly concerns. He
observes cosmic warfare — the struggle between good and evil that underlies
earthly history.

**Sixth Heaven:** The transformation intensifies. The glory is so great that
the lower heavens seem dim by comparison. Isaiah can barely distinguish himself
from the angels in this realm.

**Seventh Heaven:** Isaiah reaches the throne of God and encounters both God
the Father and the "Beloved" (the pre-incarnate Christ) as distinct yet united
presences — one of the earliest surviving texts to describe a clearly
trinitarian heavenly vision, including a clear depiction of the Holy Spirit.

### The Descent of the Beloved

The vision then describes the Beloved deliberately "descending" through each
of the heavens in disguise, transforming to look like the beings of each level
so that the lower powers do not recognize him. This descent theology — the Son
of God entering creation incognito — is one of the most sophisticated early
Christian theological ideas and influenced later Gnostic and orthodox
Christology alike.

The text then describes the full arc of the incarnation: birth, ministry,
crucifixion, resurrection, ascension, and the sending of the twelve disciples.

## Theological Significance

**Pre-existence of Christ.** The Ascension of Isaiah presents one of the
clearest early statements of Christ's eternal pre-existence and intentional
descent into humanity.

**The seven heavens.** The cosmological framework — seven ascending realms of
increasing glory — influenced medieval Christian, Jewish Kabbalistic, and
Islamic cosmology. The same structure appears in Dante's Paradiso.

**Martyrdom as witness.** The death of Isaiah establishes a template for
Christian martyrdom: the righteous prophet who speaks truth to power, suffers
without recanting, and is vindicated by God.

**Trinitarian theology.** The vision in the seventh heaven is one of the
earliest clearly Trinitarian texts outside the New Testament.

## Preservation in Ethiopia

The Ascension of Isaiah survives in its most complete form in Ge'ez. While
fragments exist in Greek, Latin, Coptic, and Slavonic, only the Ethiopian
tradition preserved the entire text. This is consistent with a pattern seen
across several Second Temple Jewish and early Christian texts: Ethiopia,
through its ancient monastic scriptoria, preserved works that were lost or
suppressed elsewhere in the Christian world.
