# Paralipomena of Baruch (4 Baruch / Rest of the Words of Baruch)

*Ethiopian Orthodox and some Eastern Orthodox canons*
*Tags: ethiopian, deuterocanon, baruch, jeremiah, apocrypha, pseudepigrapha*

## Overview

The Paralipomena of Baruch — literally "the things omitted from Baruch" — is
also known as 4 Baruch or "The Rest of the Words of Baruch." It is a Jewish
text composed around the 1st or 2nd century CE, preserved in full primarily
through the Ethiopian Orthodox and Armenian church traditions, with portions
found in Greek and Slavonic manuscripts.

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church includes it among its canonical
scriptures. In other traditions it is considered deuterocanonical or
pseudepigraphal.

## Content Summary

### Setting

The book opens at the fall of Jerusalem to the Babylonians (c. 586 BCE) and
follows Baruch, the secretary and companion of the prophet Jeremiah, and
Abimelech the Ethiopian (also called Ebed-melech in Jeremiah), a servant of
the king who had rescued Jeremiah from a cistern.

### The Sleep of Abimelech

One of the most remarkable episodes is the miraculous sleep of Abimelech.
God puts Abimelech into a deep sleep before the destruction of Jerusalem so
that he will not witness the horrors of the Babylonian conquest. He sleeps for
66 years, waking to find Jerusalem abandoned and in ruins, unaware that
decades have passed. He carries a basket of figs that have not spoiled —
a sign of divine preservation and a foreshadowing of resurrection themes.

This episode is one of the earliest clear examples in any scriptural tradition
of a "Rip Van Winkle"-type sleep used as a theological device about
preservation, time, and God's mercy.

### Jeremiah in Babylon

While Abimelech sleeps, Jeremiah accompanies the exiles to Babylon, where he
ministers to the captive community. The book details his prayers, his
communication with Baruch through letters carried by an eagle (!), and his
ongoing hope for restoration and return.

### The Eagle Messenger

A notable element: Jeremiah sends messages to Baruch via an eagle, which
delivers letters across the distance between Babylon and Jerusalem. This
unusual motif has no parallel in canonical scripture but connects to
apocalyptic and mystical literature where birds serve as divine messengers.

### Return from Exile

The narrative culminates with the return of the exiles from Babylon. However,
those who had married foreign women during the exile are required to leave
their families at the Jordan River before entering the land — a reflection of
the contentious post-exilic debates about intermarriage found in Ezra and
Nehemiah.

### Jeremiah's Death

The ending of the book is theologically striking: Jeremiah, after prophesying
about the coming of Christ (in a Christian interpolation), is stoned to death
by the people he sought to lead. He then appears to revive briefly, prophesies
once more, and dies. Some manuscripts end with Jesus himself appearing to honor
Jeremiah. This Christological ending suggests Christian authorship or editing,
likely in the 1st-2nd century CE.

## Theological Themes

**Preservation through suffering.** Abimelech's sleep and the unspoiled figs
become symbols of God's ability to preserve his people even through catastrophe.

**Hope in exile.** The book is profoundly concerned with how the faithful
community maintains hope and identity during displacement — a theme of
enduring relevance.

**Resurrection typology.** The waking of Abimelech, the reviving of Jeremiah,
and the unspoiled figs all function as types or images of resurrection.

**Prophetic continuity.** The text positions Jeremiah and Baruch as bridges
between the Hebrew Bible prophets and the coming of the Messiah.

## Significance in Ethiopian Christianity

The Paralipomena occupies a unique place in Ethiopian Christianity because it
explicitly names an Ethiopian — Abimelech — as one of the righteous figures
preserved by God during Jerusalem's darkest hour. This representation of an
African figure as a righteous servant in the innermost biblical narrative
carries deep theological meaning for Ethiopian Orthodox Christians, who see
their tradition as authentically rooted in the earliest history of God's people.
