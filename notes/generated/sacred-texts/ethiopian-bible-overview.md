# Ethiopian Orthodox Bible — Canon Overview

*Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church — 81-book canon*
*Tags: ethiopian, orthodox, canon, bible, ge'ez, tewahedo*

The Ethiopian Orthodox Tewahedo Church holds the most extensive Biblical canon
of any Christian denomination — 81 books, compared to the Protestant 66 (KJV),
Catholic 73, and Eastern Orthodox 76-79.

## Books Beyond the Protestant Canon

### Shared with Catholic / Eastern Orthodox

These books are found in the Septuagint and accepted by multiple traditions:

- **Tobit** — righteous man afflicted by blindness; angel Raphael; son Tobias
- **Judith** — Jewish widow who beheads Assyrian general Holofernes
- **1 Maccabees** — Maccabean revolt 175-134 BCE; Judas Maccabeus
- **2 Maccabees** — theological companion to 1 Maccabees; resurrection belief
- **Wisdom of Solomon** — wisdom poetry; immortality of the righteous soul
- **Sirach (Ecclesiasticus)** — practical wisdom; Ben Sira, c. 200-175 BCE
- **Baruch** — letter from Baruch; poem on wisdom; prayer of repentance
- **Prayer of Manasseh** — King Manasseh's prayer of repentance (14 verses)
- **1 Esdras (Greek Ezra)** — parallel account to Ezra-Nehemiah
- **Psalm 151** — David's song after slaying Goliath

### Additional Ethiopian Canonical Texts (liturgical / church order)

These are unique to Ethiopian Orthodoxy and primarily liturgical or canonical:
- **Sinodos** — church order and apostolic canons
- **Book of the Covenant (Mashafa Kidan)** — covenant laws
- **Ethiopic Clement** — letters attributed to Clement of Rome
- **Didascalia** — apostolic teaching document

## Why Ethiopia Has a Different Canon

The Ethiopian church was established in the 4th century CE under King Ezana
of Axum, making it one of the oldest national Christian churches. It developed
its canon largely independently of the Latin West and Greek East, drawing on
a Semitic (Ge'ez) textual tradition closely related to ancient Hebrew and
Aramaic.

The church preserved texts that were in wide use in early Jewish-Christian
communities but later excluded by Western and Eastern councils. Ethiopia's
preservation of 1 Enoch and Jubilees as fully canonical is especially
significant — both books were widely cited by early Church Fathers and
influenced the New Testament's angelology, "Son of Man" title, and apocalyptic
imagery. The Dead Sea Scrolls confirmed that 1 Enoch was widely revered in
Second Temple Judaism.

## The Ark of the Covenant

The Ethiopian Orthodox Church claims the Ark of the Covenant resides in the
Chapel of the Tablet in Axum, Ethiopia, guarded by a single monk called the
Guardian of the Ark who serves for life and is never permitted to leave.
Whether the claim is historically accurate or not, it reflects the core
Ethiopian Christian self-understanding: that Ethiopia is not peripheral to
biblical history but central to it — a claim rooted in the story of the
Queen of Sheba, her son Menelik I with Solomon, and Acts 8's account of the
Ethiopian eunuch as the first Gentile convert.
